## Findings

**F1 — Weights alone encode redundancy.** A BiLSTM reading only weight matrices prunes \~60–79% of an MNIST MLP at\<0.1–3.7pp drop. No data, no gradients needed.

**F2 — pg19's zero-shot CE screening numbers (50K-token sample) were unrepresentative; the real gap survives at proper sample size.** `zero_shot_ce_check.py`'s original 50K-token check gave GPT-2 CE=3.326 (ppl 27.83), OPT-125M CE=2.910 (ppl 18.35). Re-run at 245K tokens (matching the sweep scripts' own `n_test_tokens` default): GPT-2 CE=3.9255 (ppl 50.68), OPT-125M CE=3.4508 (ppl 31.53) — both roughly doubled, confirming pg19's book-to-book heterogeneity makes a small deterministic-streamed sample unrepresentative, not a GPT-2-specific bug. The B6-relevant part survives: gap = GPT-2_CE − OPT_CE was +0.416 nats at 50K, +0.4746 nats at 245K — same direction, similar (slightly larger) magnitude. Treat 27.83/18.35 as superseded; 50.68/31.53 are the correct zero-shot pg19 baselines to compare sweep results against.

**F3 — Pruner-capacity ablation (B7b) doesn't cleanly resolve whether high-λ underperformance is a capacity bottleneck or an inherently harder landscape — it surfaces a third, unanticipated confound: convergence-window validity is itself capacity-dependent.** `train_pruner_pg19_capacity_scaling.py` (embed_dim=128, lstm_hidden=256 — 2.257× base params; the script's own default of 144/216 was overridden for this run) vs. base-size pruner, matched at 4 λ (0.05, 0.3, 0.8, 1.6) × 2 models. Mixed result: 3/8 points improved (gpt2 λ=0.8: ppl −5.52%, fewer steps; opt125m λ=0.05: −1.19%; opt125m λ=1.6: −3.43%), 3/8 got worse (gpt2 λ=0.05: +1.80%; gpt2 λ=1.6: +1.36%; opt125m λ=0.3: **+6.22%**), 2/8 flat. No clean split by "flagged high-λ trouble point" — GPT-2 and OPT-125M disagree on which of 0.8/1.6 benefits from more capacity. The concerning case: opt125m λ=0.3 (a "should stay stable" control point) converged in *fewer* steps (900→600) at *worse* ppl, and its gap-diagnostic trajectory shows why — pct_pruned climbed 28.6%→32.2%→35.5% in a steady monotonic trend, not noise, yet still satisfied the block-mean flatness check (5%/1pp tolerance, 250-step window) and got called converged mid-climb. Mechanism: a bigger/more expressive pruner can move through weight space faster per step, so a window size tuned against the base pruner's dynamics can mistake a genuinely-still-moving trend for a plateau simply because more of the trajectory completes before the window closes. Window size and pruner capacity were both held "fixed" as the wrong assumption — window validity isn't capacity-invariant. Next step (not yet run): rerun the 2x-capacity ablation with a longer/stricter window to see if that closes the opt125m λ=0.3 gap, before drawing further conclusions about H1.

**F4 — A third capacity point (half-size pruner, tmp5) shows NO meaningful, monotonic effect of pruner capacity on %pruned, ppl quality, or convergence speed — F3's apparent "2x helps at some λ" pattern looks like noise now that there's a third point to check it against.** Three-way comparison (half=0.4678x, base=1x, 2.26x — see F3 — all at the same 4λ × 2 models = 8 matched points, 1 seed each): 0/8 points show a monotonic half<base<2.26x ordering on %pruned (avg spread 8.6% of base, non-directional); 0/8 monotonic on ppl rise (avg spread only 4.2% of base — the cleanest null of the three); steps-to-converge is the noisiest metric by far (avg spread 32.8%) but *still* only 3/8 monotonic, i.e. the metric with the most absolute variation shows the least systematic relationship to capacity. A genuine capacity effect should move the same direction from half→base→2.26x; it doesn't, anywhere, on any of the three axes. Reframes H1/B7b: within this ~5x capacity range, pruner size is not the binding constraint on outcome — effort is better spent on F3's window-validity mechanism (a fixed 250-step convergence window may not mean the same thing at different pruner sizes / different λ difficulty) than on further capacity scaling. Caveat: single seed per config throughout (as with F3) — cannot cleanly separate "truly no effect" from "a small real effect below single-seed noise," but the noise floor itself (8.6%/4.2%) is now at least characterized.

**F5 — Layer-budget vs. within-layer-selection decomposition: WITHIN-layer neuron selection is doing critical, irreplaceable work — F4's capacity-insensitivity is NOT evidence the pruner "isn't doing anything."** Motivated directly by F4's suspicious-looking null result. Architecturally the pruner's two jobs are separable (BiLSTM's `context_bias` is one scalar per layer, cannot discriminate within a layer — 100% of within-layer selection is the row-encoder's job). Test: take a trained pruner checkpoint's *exact* per-layer budget (`per_layer_kept`, the BiLSTM's discovered counts) but replace WITHIN-layer neuron choice with simple activation-magnitude ranking (same criterion as F22's baseline — mean post-ReLU activation, keep highest/prune lowest — NOT weight magnitude), same per-layer count as the real mask. Checkpoints: `tmp3/pg19_converge_sweep/{gpt2,opt125m}/lambda_0.3/pruner.pt` (base-size pruner, mid-range λ). Result: catastrophic degradation for both models — gpt2: real pruned ppl 40.698 → decomposed 87.706 (**+47.0**, worse than not pruning at all, orig=50.683); opt125m: real 35.538 → decomposed 48.707 (**+13.2**, also worse than orig=31.528). Handing the heuristic the *correct* per-layer budget (an advantage F22's own baseline never had) still fails badly — bigger gap than F22 found even. Conclusion: within-layer selection is real, substantial, necessary work a magnitude-style heuristic cannot approximate, even with perfect budget information. F4's capacity result is better read as "this particular scoring function, whatever it is, happens to be representable by a very small network (down to embed_dim=32)" — not "the row-encoder isn't computing anything meaningful."
